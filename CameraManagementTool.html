Overview

Camera Management Tool is a platform designed in the cooperation project hosted by Prof. Lin at National Taiwan University and the time-lapse photography company, Brinno. Our goal was to build a platform enabling constructors to effectively manage time-lapse cameras across multiple construction projects. With Camera Management Tool, constructors can remotely control camera settings, view camera screens, and upload and share images with their clients.

Design Process

Understand the Issue

Setting up cameras on construction sites is essential for ensuring the safety of workers and tracking the progress of the project. However, the current method for managing construction cameras is highly inefficient, as it requires constructors to check camera screens and statuses on site. This often results in delays in identifying and resolving problems, leading to captured images not meeting client expectations and legal requirements.

Research

Research Goals

  • To clarify whether the process to manage camera are consistent among different stakeholders

  • To capture users’ pain points at different stages of construction projects

Stakeholder Interview

We adopted "user journey mapping" in the interview to understand users’ behaviors, experiences, needs, and pain points in each stage of construction projects.

Key Findings

01 Users can only spot issues such as skewed or blocked camera screens onsite, often leading to unusable images

02Users struggle to frequently modify camera settings due to manpower and time shortage

03Users hope the current camera management methods can be improved to support multiple devices

04Users are often worried about camera settings being motified while sharing with their clients

How might we help constructors in efficiently managing cameras across multiple projects?

Persona

After conducting interviews, we found that photographers often take the responsibility of managing construction cameras. Therefore, we established the primary persona with them in mind.

Ideate

Drawing from Other Platforms

As I began brainstorming possible features, I realized that construction camera previews share similarities with real-time cameras used in travel and home security. Therefore, I gained valuable insights from my observations of the Zermatt Webcam and the Umbo TruePlatform.

Brainstorm Potential Features

Combining insights from research and market analysis, I revisited our persona and proposed potential platform features aligned with the 4 main user needs.

Wireframe

Wireframe v1

I then created wireframes to visualize the features that would be presented on the platform. Here's how it works: Once the user logs in, they'll be directed to the Project page, where all the cameras in a project will be displayed. By clicking on camera cards, they can view the preview and status of each camera. After that, they can navigate to the File page to upload and share files.

Wireframe v2

After discussing with the team, some adjustments were made. First, Dashboard and Device list page were added so that users can manage cameras both by projects and devices. Moreover, the perspective comparison feature was removed from the Camera Preview page due to technical constraints.

Design Iteration

File

01Upload Files

I initially proposed a design where users could upload files by clicking the upload button and dragging files into a pop-up screen. However, I refined the design based on the team's feedback that the upload area might be too small for users uploading a large number of files at once. Hence, I iterated the design to allow users to drop files directly into the page, which will be more convenient for them.

02Share Files

I presented two ways for users to share files. Considering that most clients access files via desktop, b would be a better choice.

03File List

At first, I believed that showing thumbnails would help identify unusable images. However, I soon realized that displaying images in this way made it challenging to differentiate between each image. As a result, I modified the design to focus on displaying more distinctive information, such as the file format.

Team

Step 1Explore two ways to manage project roles

I compared two common methods for managing permissions in a project. To simplify design and enhance development efficiency, we chose the role-based method.

Step 2Define roles and role permissions

I worked with the team to set the roles and permissions for our project. We identified three primary actors in most construction projects: the project owner, who has full access to all project settings; project members, who are responsible for uploading files; and clients, who can only access files. As a result, we defined project roles based on these observations.

Final Solution

Camera scheduling and preview

Remote camera scheduling and monitoring, saving users' time on on-site setup and enabling immediate anomaly detection.

File uploading and sharing

Uploading files in different formats and sharing view-only files to enhance efficient communication with clients while preventing camera settings from being accidentally altered.

Permission management

Creating three permission levels for project roles improves both internal management and external communication.

Reflection

01Deal with limitations and uncertainties in real-world scenarios

Although my knowledge of the construction field was limited and extensive user research was not feasible due to time constraints, I acquired the skill of making assumptions and adjusting them based on feedback from my team. These initial assumptions were often unclear and subject to significant changes, but they enabled me to communicate effectively with the team and ultimately reach a consensus.

02Design intuitively for users less familiar with technology

As most of our primary users were constructors who were less familiar with technology, I realized the significance of designing interfaces that were easy to use. Therefore, I stepped into their shoes and created straightforward interfaces that could help them quickly understand how to navigate the website.

03Increase domain knowledge to better understand the users

If I knew we wouldn't be able to interview many users, I would spent more time on desk research at the beginning of the project. As the project progresses, a thorough understanding of the industry will be useful in empathizing with the users.
