Overview

A useful tool for individuals with hoarding issues to declutter by determining item value

Our team created Eggy, which helps individuals with hoarding behaviors to declutter unnecessary items by determining item value in the OpenHCI workshop. Eggy allows users set their own timeframes and employs the idea of a hatching chick. When time's up, a chick and a trash bag will appear, reminding users to dispose of unused items. The growth of the chick symbolizes personal growth, helping users to better understand which possessions truly matter to them.

Problem

People struggle to let go of unnecessary items, leading to limited living spaces

In 1996, Frost and Hartl defined hoarding disorder as follows: “ (1) the acquisition of, and failure to discard a large number of possessions that appear to be useless or of limited value; (2) living spaces sufficiently cluttered so as to preclude activities for which those spaces were designed; and (3) significant distress or impairment in functioning caused by the hoarding”.[1] In modern times, sufficient supplies have given rise to hoarding, and individuals encounter the dilemma of whether to hold on or let go of items and often choose to keep them in the hope that they may be useful in the future. As hoarding intensifies, spaces become limited, resulting in burdens and challenges in life. Hence, our goal was to explore design solutions to encourage people to give away items and liberate the cluttered spaces they occupy.

Process

Go through the iterative process of design thinking

We followed the design thinking process to come up with the solution. During the ideation phase, we revisited the interview records to gain deeper insights and identify clearer problem statements.

Emphasize

Understand our users through interview

Interview

We conducted 6 interviews with people who had hoarding experience. Our goals were to :

  • Understand users’ feelings, behaviors and reasons of hoarding

  • Discover how users make decision about their behaviors toward discarding items

  • Uncover users’ needs and frustrations of hoarding tendencies

PoVs and Affinity Map

After completing interviews, we began by identifying potential PoVs (Points of View) in the interview records. We synthesized the results by listing all the needs and insights from the PoVs and identified underlying patterns using affinity mapping. By connecting related needs and insights, we were able to uncover the ones with the most connections, which in turn were regarded as the most important and relevant elements for the users.

Define

Persona

To better understand and empathize with our users, we brought them to life by creating a persona from our research findings.

User Journey Map

We went through the cleaning process with our persona to understand their feelings and behaviors in each stage. We found that when users came across items they considered useful but were unsure about their future needs, they would hesitate and prolong the cleaning process.

Ideation

How might we help users decide whether to discard items

First Iteration: Space Detector + App

  • Detect the frequencies of item usage in the area

  • Make noise when the frequencies or available space fall below a certain threshold

Try to address too many variables

Challenging for users to make changes when they are reminded passively 

Second Iteration: Decluttering Box

  • Remind users to throw away the low-usage items within a timeframe

  • Use item usage as the only criterion for determining whether to discard items

Users actively make the decision, prompting behavior change

Could the device become another possession hoarded by users?

Third Iteration: Eggy

  • Users can put items using existing space

  • Employ storytelling and interesting interactions

Align with users' needs to declutter space

Motivate users to clean up in a positive way

Reflection
What I’ve learned

This was my first time to present prototypes and interactions in non-interface format, and I enjoyed the flexibility of designing without limitations and modifying it with available resources. Furthermore, I noticed the need of iteration in design; in each iteration, we found blind spots and improved our design.

What I would have done better

In a cross-disciplinary team, I initially hesitated to present my ideas, fearing they might be wrong. However, in the process of creating the prototype, I realized that all team members had their unique roles and any opinion could contribute to the discussion. Therefore, I should confidently express my ideas and explore various possibilities without hesitation.
