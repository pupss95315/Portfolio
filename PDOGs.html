Overview

Improve the code submission process for students and teaching assistants (TAs)

PDOGs is an online judging system designed for students enrolled in courses provided by the Department of Information Management at National Taiwan University. Students can use the platform to submit coding assignments, which are then graded by TAs via the system. PDOGs had a huge update in 2020, which improved the entire experience. However, recognizing opportunities for further improvement, we refined the presentation of information and provided clearer prompts, which made it easier for students and TAs to complete tasks.

Problem

Lack of information on each page and unclear system prompts cause confusion for students and TAs

While using PDOGs, students often face difficulties due to the lack of sufficient information on each page. This leads to lengthy processes when checking information or submitting assignments. Similarly, TAs find the operations somewhat complex, and the system prompts are not clear enough. In the past, they had to rely on in-person communication with TAs from the previous semester to learn how to operate the system. As a result, we decided to redesign the platform to address the issues faced by both students and TAs.

Research

Uncover 4 problems through heuristic evaluation and interviews

1. Heuristic Evaluation for Students

As all members in the Design Team have prior experience using PDOGs, we conducted a heuristic evaluation to uncover potential areas for improvements. We found that the main challenge of the current platform was the scattered presentation of information, resulting in a time-consuming process for retrieving information and submitting assignments.

2. Interviews with TAs

We conducted interviews with 6 TAs from the two courses with the highest student enrollment. Participants were asked to showcase their regular operations on the PDOGs website, then point out areas where they had encountered inconvenience or confusion. Our goals were to:

  • Understand the behaviors, experiences and feelings of TAs using PDOGs

  • Understand the challenges encountered by TAs while using PDOGs

  • Identify areas for improvements of PDOGs

We grouped similar opinions to uncover underlying trends from research using the affinity map below. As I presented it to the development team, we further classified them as system or interface issues. The interface issues later became our primary focus.

2. Redesign sidebar

The return button in the previous system's sidebar can easily cause confusion, as it navigated to the prior layer of the sysem rather than the previous page. As a result, we added the name of the previous layer to notify users where they are shifting. Aside from that, we added icons showing the assignment's status to encourage users to submit their assignments.

4. Clearer Cues for TA

In the TA system, there are several predefined phrases and format for data input. However, the system lacks explicit prompts to guide TAs on the meaning of specific terms and system operation. Hence, we solved these issues by offering more explanations, and clearer placeholder and error messages.

Reflection
1. Enhance time management and team collaboration

In my first experience as a design leader, I was responsible for defining project goals and managing the project schedule. This was an invaluable learning experience for me, as I realized the value of ensuring that every team member comprehends their responsibilities well and incorporating the requirements and timelines of the development team into the decision-making process.

2. Design for two types of users

This project involves both student and teaching assistant roles. While these roles are not entirely conflicting, it's easy to overlook details related to one side during the design process. There's also a need to balance the importance of different features for each role.

3. Overcome constraints of established system

During the initial design phase, we came up with several ideas which were later discarded after discussing with the development team. I learned that when improving an established system, it's important to avoid getting carried away with fanciful ideas. Instead, we should focus on finding feasible enhancements within the existing framework.
